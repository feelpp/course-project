== Context

LNCMI (Laboratoire National des Champs Magnetiques Intenses) operates
resistive high-field magnets capable of producing magnetic fields up to
36 Tesla. These magnets are water-cooled polyhelix inserts composed of
concentric helical conductors carrying tens of thousands of amperes.

During operation, a monitoring system records time series of physical
quantities: magnetic field, coil currents, voltages, temperatures,
pressures, and water flow rates. Each experimental session produces a
*record* - a multivariate time series typically containing 50-70
channels sampled at regular intervals.

MagnetDB is the platform that centralizes this data. It organizes
records by *site* (a physical installation), *magnet* (an assembly of
parts), and *part* (an individual helix, ring, or current lead). The
goal of this project is to define and compute meaningful statistical
summaries of operational data at each level of this hierarchy.

== Scientific Objectives

[[obj-1-define-relevant-statistics-for-operational-records]]
=== 1. Define relevant statistics for operational records

Not all statistical summaries are equally useful for magnet operations.
The student must engage with the physical meaning of each measured
quantity to determine which statistics are scientifically meaningful.

For example:

* For the magnetic field: the max and mean are directly relevant (the
max determines the experiment's scientific reach); the ramp-up rate
matters for mechanical stress
* For temperatures: the max outlet temperature indicates thermal load;
the temperature differential (Tout - Tin) reflects cooling efficiency;
time spent above a threshold may indicate stress
* For currents: the per-coil current distribution reveals the operating
regime; the deviation between coils feeding the same power supply may
indicate an electrical issue
* For pressures and flows: these are health indicators for the cooling
system rather than scientific outputs

The student should produce a *taxonomy of statistics* organized by
physical relevance, not just by mathematical definition.

[[obj-2-compute-per-record-summaries]]
=== 2. Compute per-record summaries

For each record, compute a structured summary at three levels:

*Site level*: aggregate quantities that characterize the full
installation during this session - total power, peak field, cooling
performance, duration, duty cycle.

*Magnet level*: quantities specific to one magnet within the site -
currents for the helix power supply, field contribution, operating
regime identification (ramp-up, plateau, ramp-down, quench).

*Part level*: quantities specific to an individual helix - its current,
voltage, resistance variation (DRcoil), and calibration temperature
(Tcal). The resistance variation is particularly important: it tracks
conductor aging over the magnet's lifetime.

[[obj-3-aggregate-across-records-for-lifetime-statistics]]
=== 3. Aggregate across records for lifetime statistics

Given N records spanning the operational lifetime of a site, magnet, or
part, compute cumulative statistics:

* Total operating hours
* Lifetime max/mean/std of key quantities
* Which record holds the overall maximum (for traceability)
* Distribution of operating regimes across records
* Trend detection: is the resistance variation drifting over time? Is
the cooling efficiency degrading?

[[obj-4-exploratory-analysis]]
=== 4. Exploratory analysis

With the statistical summaries computed, investigate:

* Do operational records cluster into distinct regimes? (PCA or simple
clustering on summary vectors)
* Are there correlations between operating conditions and anomalies?
* Can you identify records that are statistical outliers?
* For parts that have been in multiple magnet configurations: does the
operating history differ?

== Mathematical Content

* *Descriptive statistics*: min, max, mean, std, skewness, kurtosis,
quantiles - applied to multivariate time series
* *Correlation analysis*: Pearson/Spearman correlation between channels
within a record; cross-correlation with time lags for causal analysis
(e.g., current increase -> temperature increase with delay)
* *Weighted aggregation*: when aggregating across records of different
durations, define appropriate weighting schemes (time-weighted means vs.
record-count means)
* *Non-stationary series*: operating records are inherently
non-stationary (ramp-up -> plateau -> ramp-down). The student must decide
whether to compute statistics over the full record, over identified
phases, or both
* *Phase identification*: simple segmentation of the time series into
operating phases using change-point detection or threshold-based rules
* *Dimensionality reduction*: PCA on the per-record summary vectors to
identify dominant modes of operational variation
* *Trend analysis*: linear regression on lifetime series of key
indicators (resistance drift, max field evolution)

== Technical Environment

=== Provided

* *Docker image* with Python 3.11, pandas, numpy, scipy, matplotlib,
seaborn, scikit-learn, Jupyter

=== Not required

* Django, MagnetDB, Docker infrastructure knowledge
* MagnetTools (not needed for raw statistics - only for Project B)
* Web development, database skills

=== Development workflow

The student works in Jupyter notebooks for exploration, then refactors
validated code into a Python module with tests. All work is done with
pandas DataFrames and Python dicts - no database interaction.

== Evaluation Criteria

* *Scientific relevance*: Are the chosen statistics physically
meaningful? Do they capture what matters for magnet operations?
* *Code quality*: Is the module well-structured, tested, documented, and
usable as a library?

patterns in the data?
* *Report quality*: Is the mathematical formulation rigorous? Are
results clearly presented?
